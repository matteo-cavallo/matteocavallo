There is a quality to things made without hurry. You can feel it in old furniture, in bread left to ferment overnight, in cities that accumulated across centuries. There is an honesty to them — each layer revealing the patience that built it.

I have been thinking about slowness. Not the kind that excuses delay, but the kind that is a commitment to care.

---

_Fast work ships._ I know this. I have shipped fast — watched features go from idea to production in days, felt the team's energy in the momentum. There is a real pleasure in that.

But I have also watched fast work accumulate. Watched codebases grow denser, harder to reason about. Watched small compromises compound into architectural problems that could not be untangled without breaking something larger.

The things that lasted — the design systems that outlived their first generation, the components still used three years later — were rarely built fastest. They were the ones where someone paused: _what problem are we actually solving? What will this need to be in two years?_

---

Slowness, done right, is not inefficiency. It is precision. It is the moment before you write the function where you ask whether the function should exist at all. It is the iteration nobody sees — the third version of an API that finally feels right, the spacing adjustment that makes a layout breathe.

Paris, where I live, understands this without trying to. Not because Parisians are slow — they are not, particularly — but because the city was built with a patience that modern urbanism cannot replicate. The Haussmann boulevards, the courtyards of the Marais, the way neighbourhoods carry memory. These things were not optimised. They accumulated. There is a difference.

---

None of this is an argument against shipping. You still have to ship. Ideas that stay perfect in your head are just ideas.

But there is a mode of building that moves deliberately — that ships, and does not rush. That accumulates quality alongside features instead of repaying it later as debt.

This site is an experiment in that mode. Some pages have taken days. I have rebuilt components I thought were finished because they did not feel quite right. It will probably never be finished — in the way that living things are not finished.

That feels right to me.

---

What I am after is the feeling of a well-made thing. The sense that someone cared enough to get it right — not just functional, but _considered_. That someone thought about the person who would use it, read it, touch it.

They made it as if it mattered.

It does. Or it can, if we let it.
